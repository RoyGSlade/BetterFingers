The smell of still smoke and old leather.
 Ritzy jazz crackled from the worn-out speakers like a memory trying to stay alive.
 A cold draft slipped through the cracks of my partner's car, brushing against my neck, reminding me the night still had teeth.
I pulled a pack of cigarettes from my jacket tattered, familiar tilted it just enough to fish one out with my lips. A flash of spark, then a deep inhale.
 The flame glowed, reflected in the window. My reflection looked back at me like a stranger I never quite trusted.
One final job. One last task. And then Goldstein would be happy.
I remember the first time I saw Freddy Goldstein. I was just some dumb kid. Hungry. Empty in that way only desperation understands.
It was sixteen years ago.
 A few of the kids from school had stumbled into a drinking den the kind of place that let you in if you knew how to shut up and keep your hands in your pockets.
The Cabbaro Pulse. A basement jazz club with no windows, no rules, and no way to know what time it was unless you counted the number of drinks spilled on your tie.
 Smoke lived in that room. Not just hung in the air lived there, curled into the corners, licked the walls. It wasn’t oppressive. It was welcoming.
 Like even the ash understood this was a place to belong if nowhere else would have you.
The music above never stopped.
 Every note from the stage upstairs seemed to seep through the old wooden floors like ghosts who still wanted to play. The laughter of musicians echoed after their sets so loud and loose you could feel their joy swelling beneath your feet.
The Cabbaro had this kind of energy, you know?
 Like nobody gave a damn what the world looked like tomorrow. Everyone was drunk on the now.
But Goldstein… he didn’t belong there. Not really.
He sat in a private booth in the back, drinking scotch that cost more than rent and smoking cigars like he invented them.
 He wasn’t loud. Didn’t need to be. His words didn’t ask for attention they pulled it, like gravity.
When he spoke, people listened. When he laughed, others smiled like they were part of an inside joke they didn’t hear.
 His presence had rhythm. Like everything else in the room had adjusted to his beat.
I wanted that.
 That quiet kind of power.
 The weight of a man who never had to raise his voice to break bones.
So I started showing up more often. Slipping through the cracks. Watching. Listening. Learning.
And one uneventful day, when nobody important was paying attention 
 I made my move.
His voice cut through the smoke like it had a blade on it.
My mouth opened… then closed. I stood there like a jackass, frozen in place for what felt like three long, humiliating minutes. Every second dragged out like it was daring me to run.
Then click I clenched my fists.
The jazz behind me, slow and sultry, started to sound like something else. I let the rhythm build in my bones, let the drums push me forward. That beat turned into my battle cry. My heart, my war drums.
“I overheard the Red Room job,” I said, jaw tight. “And I want in. I know I can help.”
The temperature in the room dropped.
“You heard about the job, huh?” Goldstein’s voice was ice with a fuse under it. He looked like he might jump across the table and carve out my tongue himself. His men shifted, tense and silent like dogs waiting for the command to bite.
For a heartbeat, I thought it was over. I saw my high school sweetheart. I saw the dreams I’d buried. All of them, lit on fire and burning up like old photographs.
But then, something deep inside me some buried piece I didn’t know was there spoke up.
“Listen,” I said, “I know the place. I used to work there. I can get you in quiet. I can make it easy.”
Goldstein stared at me like he was looking through me, like my soul was something he might have stepped in.
“What’s the deal, kid? You think you can just walk up, say the right words, and get what you want? You think you just get in on something like this?”
“No, sir,” I said, trying not to flinch. “I’m not expecting anything. I just… I want to be needed in this business. I want to be part of the future.”
That was the truth. Naked and raw.
Then like I had stepped out onto the center of a lake that had just frozen over it all cracked.
The tension shattered. Sunlight, laughter, warmth. The whole table broke into chuckles like I’d just told the punchline instead of a plea. Goldstein leaned back, exhaled, and for the first time looked more like a man than a myth. Like he’d just breathed in something fresh.
Maybe I was that breath of fresh air.
It felt good. Too good.
He let me help with that job.
Was it easy? Clean? A nice entry-level gig to get my foot in the door?
No.
No, it wasn’t.

The car door opened with a hollow clunk.
I blinked memory fading like smoke suddenly back in the now. The moment hit me like gravity after a long dream. My cigarette had burned almost all the way down between my fingers. Ash trailed to the floor mat like a fuse running low.
He was here. The guy.
My partner for the night. The one I’d been tasked to deal with.
Some relative of Goldstein’s. A nephew, maybe? Or a cousin that came from money but couldn’t hold a job longer than a steak holds heat.
I didn’t remember, and I didn’t care. What I did know was this:
He was about as useful as a lawyer without a mouth.
Or maybe worse. Maybe like a priest with no God.
His name was Rodney Wilkes.
A guy who had no business being anywhere near real work but there he was, riding shotgun through Goldstein’s inner circle like he belonged. Nepotism, probably. Or maybe Goldstein just liked keeping a few loud idiots close to feel smarter by comparison.
Either way, I had to deal with him tonight. So I was making the most of it.
The job wasn’t complicated. A simple hit. Clean and quiet. A detective named Wesley Bofinder hard-nosed, stubborn, real pain in the ass for Goldstein’s operations. He ran some half-lit, borderline-legal office down by the docks. Old files, older furniture, but he was good. Too good.
Tonight, we were going to fix that.
Trim the shrub that was Bofinder.
 Cut him down.
Or if we’re sticking with the pulp clichés make sure he “sleeps with the fishes.”
Rodney looked over at me as he slid into the seat, shutting the door like it owed him something.
“Sorry that took so long. Things in there got a little… heated,” he said. Like it was a question. Like everything he said was a question.
“You ready to head out? Need to make any stops before we get there?”
That voice. Thin, whiny, always a few decibels away from pathetic. He never just said anything he asked it. Constantly. Like the world needed to validate every word he let out.
I’d worked with him once or twice before. Never by choice.
I looked over, face blank, voice colder than steel.
“No. I’m fine.”
I tried to freeze the warmth out of the air, hoping silence would hold.
But with Rodney? Everything was always more than you asked for.

Chapter 2 – Dockside
We crossed the bridge, headlights cutting through Dockside. Though anyone who actually lived in Grimstow City never called it Dockside. To us, it was always Darkside.
Heading into Darkside, you could feel the unease settle in like fog. Hell, there usually was fog thick, heavy, like the city itself was trying to hide its secrets. The air stank of ironwork and stale water, sharp and acidic on your tongue. It made you want to turn back, had you reaching, scratching for cleaner air. But me? I always thought it gave my cigarettes that extra bit of bite.
We were close now. Close enough that familiar unease started to claw at my nerves the quiet tension right before a job. Big or small, it didn’t matter; I knew it could always go wrong, especially when the target was someone like Wesley.
The building Wesley had been occupying for the last three weeks wasn’t exactly prime real estate. It sat just under the bridge we crossed, parked barely a foot away from the most wretched concrete spill-off in all of Grimstow City.
The runoff channel itself had been a big project about ten years back   brought in a lot of jobs at the time. But now that it’s finished, it stands as one of the city’s biggest excuses for avoiding real change, especially here in Darkside.
It collects everything: factory waste, sewage, rainwater   and the stench is exactly what you'd imagine. Add to that the fact Goldstein’s crew and half the other local outfits have been dumping bodies in it for years like it’s the city’s unofficial graveyard, and you’ve got a smell that never stops giving.
Luckily for us, the mayor turns a blind eye every damn year.
When the press tries to question Mr. Harvey about the missing persons reports, or the constant sludge our factories dump into that channel, well… let’s just say there’s a reason we put him in office.
The absence of life wasn’t irregular in Darkside. It was quite simple, really.
 Here’s a place in the city where all the filth and bile comes to be slowly eaten away by that natural decay   the kind that only happens to things easily forgotten, or things that already smell like shit.
As we drove through, a smattering of workers stood waiting for their bus   just off their shift.
 They knew, same as I did, that there’d come a time when they’d be flushed too.
 Covered in dirt. Worked to the bone. Eager to get back to some shabby studio apartment where they’d lie down on a yellowed mattress and brace for another day of this misery they called life.
Poor, honest schmucks.
I have pity for them.
I know they’ve never tasted the good life   the kind that comes with a $300 bottle of wine and a true Cuban cigar.
 That smoke hits different. That glass of red rewrites a man’s posture. His step changes. His voice slows.
 Once you’ve had that kind of night, people stop talking over you.
That’s the whole sense of the thing down here   anguish and poverty.
 I could almost drown in it.
Desperation doesn’t just sit on their backs. It pulls them down.
 It drags them until they’re suffocating in it   and when their bodies finally break, they don’t even have to crawl far.
 They just roll into the city’s concrete spill-off   that oversized gutter   and let it carry them home.
When the workers and the streets share the same grime, that’s when you know:
 It’s the city itself that’s dragging these men to their knees.
Darkside felt closer to death than anywhere else in Grimstow.
I hated it.
Most of the drive down here, I gave Rodney the ice version of myself short answers, shorter patience, no eye contact, no body language to suggest I was even remotely content with being his ride-along on this gig.
 He reached under his coat, grabbing his piece a simple snub-nose pistol.
 “I think I’m ready. I just want to get this over with,” he said.
The address for Detective Wesley’s covert office came from one of our inside guys. Supposedly, Wesley had been working to take us down from here for a few weeks. Unfortunately for him, he didn’t work fast enough.
Rodney wouldn’t quit like a goddamn mosquito hungry for blood. That’s the thing: I didn’t like him. I knew he might have potential in this line of work, but I hated the kind of guy who would drain the life out of you before ever evolving into a better enforcer.
I felt like hitting him in the head. This jackass is gonna get us both killed.
Rodney’s a waste of space. Sure, sometimes you start out that way and make something of yourself. But this bag of empty wind? I couldn’t see it happening.
We crossed the street, shadows stretching across the asphalt. Wesley’s office was an offshoot door, just adjacent to the main building. I tried the knob. Locked.
“Fuck. Is he even here?” Rodney muttered, mosquito-voiced and persistent. The guy wouldn’t quit.
“Listen, this shit happens. If he’s not here, that’s better for us. I’ll pick the lock then we get in and set up an ambush.”
 Out of my pocket where some guys keep a handkerchief I always carry a tension wrench and pick.
Rodney shot me a look. “The hell kinda enforcer are you?” he muttered.
“A good one, jackass. Now shut up.” I jutted my chin, not in the mood for his questions. Actually, probably not but I wasn’t about to admit it.
Lock clicked.
At least Rodney followed. Instead of sitting in the car like dead weight, he stuck with me. Maybe he had some spine after all. Or maybe he was just too stupid to know when to quit.
We went up the stairs. The air clung to the walls, musty and thick. At the top, another door. Unlocked. I opened it fast, hands free, just in case anyone tried to jump us.
 We moved through the top floor, slow and methodical. The place felt sloppy. If Wesley was gone for good, he’d have cleaned up hidden notes, burned files. Instead, he left a map, open cases, evidence boards. Like he stopped caring, or something yanked him out too quick.
Living room clear. A few desks, open files. A weird map set up on the board Grimstow, marked and sectioned off like a war plan. Lines drawn in red. Photos pinned. Notes scribbled in angry handwriting. All still here.
I pushed on.
The kitchenette quiet. Dining table cluttered with trashy takeout and old newspaper clippings. An old coffee cup, half full, black as engine oil. Cigarette ash overflowed the tray, burned into the glass.
 Looked like someone might’ve been shot in here. Blood splatter. Hard to say how old for sure. But it smelled like something bad had happened and recently, too. Or maybe my nose was just telling me what I wanted to believe.
Two more doors bedroom and bathroom.
“Take the bathroom,” I told Rodney. He nodded, disappearing behind the door.
I went through the bedroom. Not much to find. Bed barely looked used. One pillow, flat and yellowed. Closet held a few pairs of clothes just enough for a quick change. No photos. No keepsakes. No warmth. Just cold utility.
Then I heard water.
“Rodney,” I called, stepping toward the sound. “What the hell are you doing?”
I rounded the corner to find him splashing cold water on his face in the sink. He wiped off with a towel, then glanced over like I’d interrupted something private.
He was unraveling, like just prepping for this ambush was enough to undo him or maybe he was worried about something else. No one was even here.
I could feel the tension rising in my throat. Ready to tell him to pull it together.
He spoke first.
 “Sorry, I’m just my stomach’s been in knots since we crossed the bridge. Feels like something’s tearing me up from the inside. I just needed a second to breathe.”
That stopped me for half a second. He wasn’t just nervous. He was unraveling, coming apart at the seams, even with an empty apartment.
Didn’t matter. You’re still in the sink, jackass, I reminded myself. Doesn’t matter what you’re feeling. You don’t get to relax here.
But the doubt was planted.
Something’s off.
I drifted toward the windows. Two of them. One faced the street headlights flickering down the road, a few cars creeping past. Nothing unusual.
 The other looked out over the dam. Peaceful, almost. Like the world had forgotten this place existed.
I turned back and Rodney had a gun pointed straight at me.
“You know, Louie… I’ve always looked up to you,” he said, voice barely steady, but cold underneath. “And I’m sorry to do this to ya.”
He didn’t blink.
“Boss said only one of us gets the promotion,” he added, shrugging like it was just business. “Hate to say it, but… it’s gonna be me.”
The last thing I saw? Rodney, standing there with a dumbshit smirk across his face. That goddamn face.
Then 
 CRACK.
The shot rang out. The sound hit me before the pain did. And I fell backward into the dark.
Then nothing.
 No pain. Just black.
For a second, I thought maybe that was it. Lights out. Fade to black. Roll credits on Louie.
But then...
First thought:
There’s a god out there, and if he’s real, he sure as hell ain’t happy with me.
Second thought:
 Fucking Rodney.
Third thought:
 Amelia.
 Goddamn it. She’s gonna get herself killed without me around.


Chapter 3 – "Ash & Steel"
Louis woke coughing, lungs choked by smoke that wasn't there. The air felt thick, tarred, infused with a gritty scent of smoldering garbage and burnt oil. Consciousness returned in splintered pieces: cold pavement pressing into his spine, a grimy dampness clinging to his skin, and the mournful wail of jazz spilling from a nearby radio, distorted into surreal loops by speakers hanging onto life by threads of rust and wire.
He opened his eyes slowly. Neon lights blinked overhead, jaundiced and irregular, painting streaks of sickly color in puddles around him. The buildings tilted impossibly, warped reflections of familiar alleys and storefronts Dockside twisted out of shape, seen through fevered eyes.
"Dead," he murmured softly. It felt true, tasted like truth, yet his heart protested violently beneath his ribs. He brushed trembling fingers across his chest, expecting blood and pain. Instead, he found hardened flesh, a strange new ridge marking his skin. With cautious fingers, Louis tugged his shirt open, revealing a freshly healed scar: barrel-shaped, precise, and impossible.
Nearby, the crackling flames in a rusty barrel threw dancing shadows against brick walls, alive with the warmth of embers. A hunched figure stood over the flames, prodding the glowing ashes with an iron rod. The figure coughed harshly, a deep rasp shaking the air, carrying years of smoke and decay.
“Thought you’d sleep forever,” came a gravelly voice, seasoned and deep, flavored by whisky and weary laughter. The old man straightened slightly, illuminated by firelight. His gray beard spilled out beneath the brim of a battered fedora, eyes glinting with mischief, reflecting flames as if they burned behind his gaze. "Not everyone survives their first taste of smoke."
Louis propped himself up weakly, vision swirling with dizziness. "Who...who are you?"
The old man chuckled quietly, poking at something charred over the fire a rat, perhaps, skewered on rusted rebar. “Ah, names are funny things, kid. You can call me Father Time, though folks have called me far worse.”
Louis shook his head, disoriented. The alley seemed infinite, walls stretching upward into shadowy oblivion, echoes dancing endlessly. He rubbed at the scar, confused, desperate for clarity. "Am I dead, then?"
"Dead?" Father Time smiled wryly, as though amused by a joke Louis couldn't hear. "No, not yet. Close enough to smell it, maybe. But the reaper hasn’t decided if you're worth the trouble." He turned the rat slowly, fat dripping into the flames with sizzling pops. "Death, you’ll find, is particular about who she dances with."
Louis grimaced, pressing a hand to his forehead. Memories surged Rodney, the betrayal, the flash of gunfire. "Rodney shot me," he whispered bitterly.
The old man's gaze darkened briefly, voice gentle yet firm, resonating like distant thunder. "He shot your body, sure. But it takes more than bullets to kill what's real in a man." Father Time leaned forward, eyes sharp beneath bushy brows. "Ash and steel that's what you've been marked by. Ash reveals truth, while steel binds it. You, Louis, are caught between both."
Anger and frustration churned in Louis’s chest. "I don't have time for riddles."
The old man laughed heartily, a rumble rolling through the alley. "Time? Kid, you have exactly as much as you choose to make." His eyes softened, voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. "Riddles might save you yet."
Louis stood shakily, leaning against damp brick, senses overloaded by the strangeness around him. "How am I supposed to understand any of this?"
Father Time considered him seriously, face illuminated by dancing flames. "The world’s a puzzle, Louis. Each piece connects to another. And here’s a truth to start you on your way your answers lie with friends you no longer trust. Alone, you'll drown in shadows. Together, you might just glimpse the light."
A sudden wind whipped through the alley, scattering embers and chilling Louis to the bone. The neon lights sputtered violently. He felt dizzy again, his vision darkening at the edges, shadows pooling like spilled ink.
Father Time stepped closer, gently placing a gnarled hand on Louis’s shoulder. The touch sent warmth cascading through his body, pushing back the cold, fighting back the shadows. "Keep your friends close," the old man murmured softly. "But keep your scars closer. They’ll remind you who you are."
Louis reached to reply, but the alley spun around him, collapsing into darkness. Father Time’s voice faded, blending into jazz notes that cracked and skipped, spinning into silence.
"Remember, Louis open the doors, or they will open you."
And then, abruptly, it was gone.
Louis snapped awake again, gasping. He lay sprawled across familiar pavement, gritty and unyielding. Dockside appeared exactly as it had always been dreary, rain-streaked, and coldly indifferent. The barrel was gone, the flames extinguished, the jazz silenced as though it had never existed. Father Time had vanished, leaving nothing behind except the aching memory of his cryptic warnings and a heavy certainty lodged deep in Louis’s chest.
Cautiously, Louis lifted his shirt again. The scar remained, stark and real, a tangible reminder of an impossible encounter. His heart quickened. The world he knew had shifted, twisted just enough to be noticed but not explained.
Ash and steel, truth and binding riddles within riddles.
A sudden noise broke his thoughts footsteps on wet pavement. Louis's hand instinctively reached for his weapon, finding only empty space. He clenched his jaw, forcing back panic, and turned slowly toward the sound.
From the shadows emerged a familiar face battered, wary, but unmistakably alive.
"Jesus, Louis. Thought Rodney finished you for sure," came the voice of a friend Louis hadn't expected to see. The man stepped closer, half-smiling, cautious. "Guess we both got lucky tonight."
Relief and suspicion mingled inside Louis. Father Time’s words echoed again trust and doubt warring violently in his mind. He hesitated, then finally extended his hand. "Lucky's one way to put it. I'm starting to think the universe has its own twisted sense of humor."
"Yeah, welcome to Dockside," the friend answered, gripping Louis's hand firmly. "Nothing makes sense here not really. But maybe, if we’re lucky, we can figure it out together."
Louis nodded slowly, eyes distant, feeling the weight of the scar like a promise branded onto his soul.
Together, he thought, remembering the warmth of Father Time’s hand and the enigmatic advice given beneath flickering neon lights. If ash reveals and steel binds, maybe trust was the key to both.
He took a steadying breath, straightened his shoulders, and stepped into the shadows, uncertain yet resolved, carrying the knowledge that nothing was quite as it seemed and that sometimes, understanding began with embracing what felt impossible.
